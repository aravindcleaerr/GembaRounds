Part I — Gemba Walk Fundamentals

1 What is Gemba Walk?

Gemba (現場) is a Japanese term meaning "the actual place" — the real location where work happens and value is created. In manufacturing, this is the shop floor.

A Gemba Walk is a structured management practice where leaders physically go to the workplace to:

  • Observe actual processes as they happen (not from reports)
  • Engage with workers who do the work every day
  • Identify opportunities for improvement, waste, and safety hazards
  • Appreciate good practices and recognize effort
  • Act on findings by assigning accountability and tracking closure
"Go see, ask why, show respect." — Taiichi Ohno, Father of the Toyota Production System
Important Distinction

A Gemba Walk is NOT an audit, inspection, or blame exercise. It is about understanding reality, connecting with people, and building a culture of continuous improvement. The moment workers feel "inspected" rather than "supported," they stop sharing real problems.

Origin & History

Gemba Walks originated at Toyota Motor Corporation in Japan as part of the Toyota Production System (TPS) in the 1950s-70s. Taiichi Ohno, the architect of TPS, insisted that managers could not understand problems by sitting in offices reading reports — they had to go to the genba (the actual place) and see for themselves.

The practice spread globally through Lean Manufacturing and is now considered a cornerstone of operational excellence in manufacturing, healthcare, logistics, and service industries.

The Problem Gemba Walks Solve

Current State in Most Factories

73% of manufacturing issues go unreported. Managers spend 80% of their time in offices, making decisions based on second-hand information. The shop floor remains invisible.

3 COACH Framework (PSS Methodology)

The COACH framework, developed by PS Satish, provides a structured, repeatable approach to Gemba walks. Each letter represents a critical step that ensures walks are comprehensive and people-centered.

C
Connect
Meet operating people on the floor. Build rapport and trust.
O
Observe
See the actual process. Don't rely on reports.
A
Appreciate
Acknowledge good work. Positive reinforcement.
C
Conform
Check if standards and SOPs are followed.
H
Honing
Highlight improvements. Tag for action.

Detailed COACH Guide

StepWhat to DoWhat to RecordIn GembaRounds
C — Connect Greet workers by name. Ask how they are. Inquire about challenges they face. Listen actively. Build trust through regular presence. "Person Met" field COACH tracker lights up "C" when Person Met is filled
O — Observe Walk the floor with fresh eyes. Look at the process flow, material movement, machine conditions. Watch for safety hazards, cleanliness, organization. Don't rely on reports. Observation title + description COACH tracker lights up "O" when first observation recorded
A — Appreciate Actively look for good things. Clean workstation? Good 5S? Properly displayed SOP? Say it out loud. Write it down. This is the most important step — it builds the trust needed for the other steps to work. Positive observations (Good Thing toggle) COACH tracker lights up "A" when a positive observation is added
C — Conform Check standards adherence: Are SOPs displayed at eye level? Are log sheets filled? Are gauges calibrated? Is FIFO followed? Are safety guards in place? Area: "Adherence to Standards" COACH tracker lights up "C" when observation area is Standards
H — Honing For every improvement needed: clearly define the issue, tag the responsible person, set expectation for resolution. Follow up in the next walk or weekly review. "Person Tagged" + negative observation COACH tracker lights up "H" when both conditions met
Key Insight from PSS

The order matters. Always Connect and Appreciate before Conforming and Honing. If you start with criticism, you lose trust. If you start with connection and appreciation, people are open to improvement feedback.

5 7 Wastes of Lean (Muda) — TIMWOOD

The 7 wastes (Muda) are the foundation of Lean Manufacturing. They represent activities that consume resources but add no value for the customer. Gemba walks are the most effective way to spot them.

LetterWasteDefinitionWhat to Look ForImpact
TTransportUnnecessary movement of materialsMaterials traveling long distances, criss-crossing the floor, double-handlingTime, damage risk, cost
IInventoryExcess stock beyond what's neededPiles of WIP, overflowing bins, raw material sitting unused for weeksCash tied up, space, obsolescence
MMotionUnnecessary movement by workersOperators walking far for tools, bending, reaching, searching for itemsFatigue, time, ergonomic injury
WWaitingIdle time while waiting for somethingWorkers standing idle, machines waiting for material/programs/approvalLost productivity, cost
OOver-productionMaking more than customer demandProducing ahead of schedule, batch sizes larger than neededInventory cost, space, rework risk
OOver-processingDoing more work than the customer requiresExtra inspections, polishing unseen surfaces, unnecessary tight tolerancesTime, cost, no value added
DDefectsProducts that don't meet specificationRejection bins, rework stations, customer complaint boardsCost, delivery, reputation
Mnemonic: TIMWOOD

Transport • Inventory • Motion • Waiting • Overproduction • Overprocessing • Defects

Some practitioners add an 8th waste: Non-Utilized Talent (TIMWOODS) — underusing people's skills, knowledge, and creativity.

6 Walk Frequency & Duration Guide

LevelWho Should WalkFrequencyDurationFocus
Shop FloorSupervisors, Team LeadersDaily15-20 min5S, safety, machine status, immediate issues

LeadershipMD, CEO, Board MembersMonthly1-2 hoursOrganization-level practices, employee engagement, vision
Golden Rule

Consistency is more important than duration. A 15-minute daily walk creates more lasting impact than a 2-hour walk done once a month. The key is to make Gemba walks a non-negotiable habit.

Scheduling Best Practices

  • Block Gemba walk time in your calendar — treat it like a meeting
  • Vary the time of day — morning, mid-shift, and end-of-day reveal different issues
  • Rotate areas — don't always walk the same route
  • Use GembaRounds' recurring scheduler to auto-create walks
  • Never cancel a Gemba walk for a meeting — the floor IS the meeting

7 Best Practices & Common Mistakes

Best Practices (DO)

#PracticeWhy
1Go with an open mind — observe, don't judgeJudging shuts down communication
2Talk to operators — ask "what problems do you face?"Workers know the real issues better than anyone
3Appreciate good work publicly and immediatelyPositive reinforcement is the strongest behavior modifier
4Take notes/photos immediatelyMemory fades within hours; evidence supports action
5Follow up on previous observationsShows that you take findings seriously; builds credibility
6Vary your route every timeDifferent paths reveal different issues
7Walk at different times (morning, mid-shift, late)Issues vary by time of day and shift
8Include operators in solutionsThey have the best ideas for their own processes

Common Mistakes (DON'T)

#MistakeConsequence
1Treating it as an audit or inspectionWorkers hide problems instead of sharing
2Going only when there's a crisisWalk becomes associated with punishment
3Skipping the appreciation stepWorkers feel only negative things are noticed
4Making promises you can't keepTrust erodes; workers stop engaging
5Spending all time in one areaOther areas feel neglected; issues missed
6Jumping to solutions on the spotQuick fixes often don't address root cause
7Not following up on actionsWalk becomes a ritual with no impact
8Delegating walks to junior staff onlyLoses the "management goes to the floor" principle

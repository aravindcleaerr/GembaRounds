Case Study 1: Precision Components Pvt Ltd

CNC Machining  |  200 Employees  |  6-Month Engagement

The Problem

Precision Components Pvt Ltd ran a CNC machining operation serving the aerospace and industrial sectors. Every month, their safety team flagged two to three recurring incidents — a tool-holding mishap here, an unguarded chip ejection there. Despite post-incident reviews, the same hazards reappeared the following month.

The root cause was structural: observations were informal, action items were scribbled on paper and rarely followed up, and there was no visibility into whether corrective actions had actually been implemented. Safety became reactive — always chasing the last incident rather than preventing the next one.

Key Pain Point

Without documented, trackable action closure, the same safety gaps repeated cycle after cycle. Monthly incident reviews became a demoralising ritual with no lasting change.

The Solution

The plant manager launched daily Gemba walks using GembaRounds, led by shift supervisors and reviewed weekly by the plant head. Walks focused on the Safety and Machine Condition observation areas from the PSS 15-area framework. The COACH structure ensured every walk ended with captured observations, assigned actions, and a named owner with a due date.

  • Walk frequency: Daily (supervisors, 15 min) + Weekly (plant head, 30 min)
  • Observation areas: Safety, Machine Condition, House Keeping
  • Framework: COACH — Connect, Observe, Appreciate, Conform, Honing
  • Accountability: Every action assigned to a named owner with a target closure date

Case Study 2: AutoParts Manufacturing

Auto Components  |  500 Employees  |  4-Month Engagement

The Problem

AutoParts Manufacturing had been running 5S audits for over two years. The scores hovered stubbornly between 58% and 62% — never breaking through. The reasons were familiar to any lean practitioner: no ownership, no follow-through, no photo evidence to hold areas accountable.

Managers would walk through, note observations verbally, and move on. By the next week the improvement had eroded — partly because workers didn't feel accountable, and partly because managers didn't have records to refer back to. The 5S culture remained surface-level.

Key Pain Point

Without photo evidence and digital records, 5S observations had no teeth. Areas improved before an audit and reverted the day after. Accountability existed on paper only.

The Solution

Department managers committed to weekly Gemba walks using GembaRounds, each walk focused on the House Keeping (5S) observation area. Photo evidence was captured in-app at each station. Action items flowed directly to area owners with a target date. The weekly walk summary was shared with the team in Friday briefings — recognising areas that improved and following up on those that had not.

  • Walk frequency: Weekly (department managers, 30 min per area)
  • Observation areas: House Keeping (5S), Standards, Discipline
  • Photo evidence: Captured per station, linked to action items
  • Employee engagement: Best-5S-area recognition in weekly briefings

Case Study 3: MedEquip Industries

Medical Devices  |  150 Employees  |  Quality-Critical Environment

The Problem

MedEquip Industries manufactured precision measurement and therapeutic equipment for the medical sector — a domain where gauge calibration is not optional. Despite clear procedures, calibration due-dates were being missed. Gauges past their calibration date were found in use on the production floor. Two quality escapes had reached a customer in one quarter, triggering a corrective action request and significant rework costs.

The root cause was monitoring: calibration records lived in a spreadsheet that nobody checked proactively. There was no floor-level mechanism to catch an out-of-date gauge before it was used.

Key Pain Point

A spreadsheet nobody opens is not a system. Calibration oversight existed on paper but had no connection to what was actually happening on the production floor day-to-day.

The Solution

MedEquip focussed every Gemba walk on the "Gauges and Fixture" observation area — one of the 15 PSS areas built into GembaRounds. Daily walks by supervisors specifically checked calibration stickers, due dates, and condition of measuring instruments. Any gauge within 7 days of its calibration due date was logged as an observation, immediately triggering a notification to the quality team.

  • Walk frequency: Daily (supervisors, 10 min — gauge-focussed walk)
  • Observation area: Gauges & Fixture (primary), Machine Condition (secondary)
  • Early warning: Observations logged >7 days before due date sent automated alerts
  • Closure verification: Quality team confirmed calibration before closing the action

Key Takeaways

These three case studies span very different industries and problem types — yet the underlying lessons are the same:

Takeaway 1: Structure beats frequency

A structured 15-minute walk with the COACH framework yields more actionable insight than an unstructured hour-long floor tour. The discipline of the observation area checklist keeps walks focused.

Takeaway 2: Digital records create accountability

When observations are captured digitally with a named owner and a due date, closure rates improve dramatically. The action item doesn't disappear into a paper notebook — it stays visible until resolved.

Takeaway 3: Photo evidence changes behaviour

Photographs are objective. When workers know their area can be photographed and compared week-over-week, standards are maintained between walks — not just for the walk itself.

Takeaway 4: Proactive beats reactive

MedEquip's gauge story illustrates the core value proposition: catching a gauge 7 days before its due date costs minutes. Dealing with a quality escape costs weeks. The Gemba walk is the mechanism that makes early detection systematic.

Takeaway 5: Culture follows systems

AutoParts saw a 200% increase in employee-initiated suggestions — an outcome nobody planned for. When workers see that observations lead to real action and recognition, they start contributing. The tool enables the culture; the culture amplifies the results.
